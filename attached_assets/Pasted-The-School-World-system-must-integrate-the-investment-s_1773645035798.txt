The School World system must integrate the investment simulator directly with the classroom economy so the platform functions like a realistic financial environment rather than separate tools. Any profit earned by students within the investment simulator must automatically transfer into their School World economy balance, allowing the money they earn through investing to become real spendable currency within the platform. This ensures that financial learning and financial rewards are connected, meaning students experience the outcomes of successful or unsuccessful investment decisions. If a student earns profits from buying and selling stocks in the simulator, those profits should immediately increase their School World balance and become money they can save, spend, or invest further. The School World economy should simulate real financial systems so students learn how money behaves in real life. Students should be able to spend their earnings within a built-in marketplace where they can purchase assets such as housing, businesses, vehicles, or other meaningful items that represent ownership and long-term value. Certain assets should generate passive income over time while others may require ongoing costs such as maintenance, taxes, or utilities, forcing students to make real financial decisions about how they manage their money. The system should also support savings accounts where students can store money and earn interest over time, teaching the concept of saving and compound growth. Students should also have the ability to take optional loans which must be repaid with interest, helping them understand borrowing, debt, and financial responsibility. To make the financial system more realistic, the platform should calculate and display each student’s net worth by combining their available cash balance, their investment portfolio value from the simulator, and the value of any assets or businesses they own. A financial dashboard should allow students to clearly see their income sources, expenses, investments, owned assets, passive income, debts, and total net worth so they can understand how wealth grows over time rather than focusing only on short-term spending. The economy should also run automated financial mechanics in the background such as recurring rent payments, utility expenses, savings interest payouts, passive income from businesses, and random financial events that simulate real economic situations like bonuses, unexpected costs, market shifts, or opportunities. These background systems allow the financial world to feel alive and continuously running even when teachers are not manually controlling it. At the same time, teachers must retain powerful administrative tools that allow them to guide and customize the classroom economy. Teachers should be able to create classroom jobs that provide small consistent income, assign rewards or expenses, run auctions where students can bid on rewards or privileges, create marketplace items, trigger special economic events, and monitor student financial progress including balances, investments, and assets. These teacher controls should enhance the automated economy rather than replace it so the platform always functions as a living financial simulation while still allowing educators to connect activities to their lessons. The final goal is to create a realistic financial learning environment where students earn money through education and investing, manage finances through spending and ownership, build wealth through smart decisions, and experience both the opportunities and responsibilities that come with money. By connecting the investment simulator with asset ownership, passive income, financial responsibilities, automated economic systems, and teacher-guided classroom activities, School World becomes a fully interactive financial simulation that teaches students how real-world money management works while remaining engaging, competitive, and educational.
